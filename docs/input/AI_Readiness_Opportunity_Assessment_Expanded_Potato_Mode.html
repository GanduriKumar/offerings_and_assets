4 — What The Product Must Actually Be

This product is not a training, not a discovery workshop series, and not a generic AI strategy review. It must be a decision product that tells a leadership team whether their AI effort is built to scale or built to stall.

Core definition

A fixed-scope executive diagnostic that identifies the structural decisions preventing an enterprise AI programme from becoming commercially useful, operationally governable, and scalable.

What it should explicitly assess

  • Use-case quality: are they chasing demos or value-bearing workflows?
  • Operating-model readiness: who owns what, how decisions get made, where execution breaks.
  • Governance readiness: policy, control, auditability, accountability, and escalation design.
  • Context readiness: whether AI systems actually have the information architecture, retrieval discipline, and workflow context to be reliable.
  • Production readiness: observability, failure handling, measurement, and trust posture.
  • Commercial readiness: whether the programme can be defended in terms of cost, risk reduction, growth, or productivity.

In plain English: this product tells the buyer what is broken, what matters most, what to fix first, and whether scaling is sensible right now.

7 — How To Build The Method So It Survives Scrutiny

If this product becomes real, it will be challenged in three directions: executives will ask if it is practical, technical leaders will ask if it is rigorous, and procurement will ask if it is differentiated. The method has to withstand all three.

Minimum method components

  1. A clear assessment model with 5–7 dimensions only. Example: use cases, operating model, governance, context architecture, production controls, measurement, commercial value.
  2. Observable signals for each dimension so the scoring is not based on vibes. Example: documented ownership, escalation paths, evaluation criteria, monitoring approach, decision rights, business KPI linkage.
  3. Evidence collection pattern that is lightweight but credible: interviews, artefact review, system/workflow review, and programme documentation.
  4. A prioritisation logic that ranks issues by consequence, urgency, and fixability.
  5. A consistent output format that becomes recognisable IP: one-page summary, scorecard, risk/opportunity map, 90-day action roadmap.

What not to do

  • Do not invent a giant maturity framework with 47 sub-dimensions.
  • Do not claim scientific precision you cannot defend.
  • Do not make the scoring so soft that every client lands at "moderately mature." That is consulting wallpaper.
  • Do not over-index on tooling. Buyers pay for decision clarity, not for tool taxonomy.

8 — How To Deliver It Without Drifting Into Low-Value Work

  1. Time-box it aggressively. Two weeks is good. Three weeks is tolerable. Six weeks means you are doing consultancy sprawl.
  2. Cap stakeholder interviews. Too many interviews makes the product expensive to deliver and politically messy.
  3. Standardise the findings structure. Every client should get roughly the same output architecture, even if findings differ.
  4. Separate diagnosis from implementation. The assessment says what matters and what to do next. It does not include implementation labour unless separately contracted.
  5. Refuse vague workshop add-ons inside the base scope. That is where margins go to die.
  6. Anchor every finding to business consequence. If a finding does not affect cost, speed, trust, scale, risk, or revenue, it probably should not be in the top layer of the output.

Rule: the product should leave the client clearer, not busier.
